Conjunctions

  • Definition

    Conjunctions are words that link or join words, phrases, and clauses together.

    For example:
    Jasmine and Kendra rowed the boat across the pond.
    The computer is not working right, or Jonathan is simply frustrated.

    Conjunctions join words, phrases, and clauses to form compounds.
    There are four types of conjunctions

    Correlative conjunctions Two-word conjunctions that also coordinate, but they must be used together
    Subordinate conjunctions words that start dependent or subordinate clauses and link them to the main clause

  • Coordinating

    Coordinating Conjunctions

    Coordinating conjunctions form compounds, a term used in grammar to signify more than one item.

    For example:
    Brian and Jason enjoy swimming in the summer. (compound subject)
    Elizabeth drafted an excellent first draft or thoroughly revised his term paper. (compound verb)
    During her game Zoie tried to hit the ball, but it was just coming too fast. (compound sentence)
    Eli went inside, so he would not get wet. (compound sentence)

    Coordinating Conjunctions
    and but or (nor when negative)
    so yet for (when it means because)
  • Correlative

    Correlative Conjunctions

    Correlative conjunctions work in pairs to connect two equal words, phrases, and clauses.

    For example:
    The colors of the house not only create a fresh look but also enhance the color of the roof. (compound verb)
    Anna loves both language class and math class. (compound direct object)
    Edie would like to have either a boy or a girl. (compound direct object)
    Grandma gave neither Jackson nor Eli a treat because of their behavior. (compound indirect object)

    Correlative conjunctions
    both...and neither...nor
    either...or not only....but also

    There are three uses of correlative conjunctions you should be careful of when you use them:


    When you use correlative conjunctions, be careful about verb agreement.

    If you connect two subjects with a correlative conjunction, the second one must agree with the verb that follows.

    For example:
    Every single evening either the horned owl or the squabbling cats wake Samantha with their racket.
    Every single evening either the squabbling cats or the horned owl wakes Samantha with its racket.

    When you use correlative conjunctions, be careful about pronoun agreement.
    If you connect two antecedents with a correlative conjunction, the second one must agree with the pronoun that follows.

    For example:
    Neither Keylen nor the cousins expressed their disappointment when blind Aunt Sophie set down the plate of burnt hamburgers.
    Neither the cousins nor Yolanda expressed her disappointment when blind Aunt Sophie set down the plate of burnt hamburgers.

    When you use correlative conjunctions, be careful about parallel structure.
    Either ... or, neither ... nor, and not only ... but also require special attention when you are proofreading for parallelism. Be sure that you have equal grammatical units after both parts of the conjunction.

    For example:
    You can have two main clauses like this:
    Not only did Michael grill a steak for Tiffany, but he also prepared a hotdog for Rocket, her dog.
    Or you can shorten the sentence with two prepositional phrases:
    Michael grilled meat not only for Tiffany but also for Rocket, her dog.
    Or you can have two nouns as this version does:
    Michael grilled meat for not only Tiffany but also Rocket, her dog.

  • Subordinate

    Subordinate Conjunctions

    Subordinate conjunctions are used to begin subordinate, or dependent, adverbial clauses.

    For example:

    Though Zoie fed the fish regularly, they continued to eat any new fish she added to the bowl.
    We are planning a cookout for fathers day even if it is going to rain.

    Subordinate Conjunctions
    after in order that though
    although if till
    as if only unless
    as if now that until
    as long as once when
    as though rather than whenever
    because since where
    before so that whereas
    even if than wherever
    even though that while
  • Conjunctive

    Conjunctive Adverbs

    Conjunctive adverbs are special adverbs used to join words, phrases, and sentences together to show a relationship between them.
    They provide smooth transitions from one idea to another and clarify what the writer is trying to communicate.

    A conjunctive adverb can join two main clauses. Just like coordinating conjunctions, conjunctive adverbs connect two main ideas; however, the writer uses a semi-colon instead of a comma preceding the conjunctive adverb and a comma after.
    main clause + ; + conjunctive adverb + , + main clause.

    For example:
    When the earthquake struck, some buildings fell ; furthermore, the streets contained huge gaping holes.

    Conjunctive Adverbs
    actually furthermore on the other hand
    also however otherwise
    as a result in addition secondly
    besides in fact similarly
    consequently in short subsequently
    conversely in the third place then
    for example likewise therefore
